# TaskFlow — Design Context

## Users

Self-aware, intentional adults aged 18+. Already tracking something — gym, habits, budgets — but frustrated by tool sprawl. Not first-timers. People who have opted into self-improvement and want a system that connects the dots. They open TaskFlow each morning wanting to feel like they have a **command center**, not a cheerleader.

**Emotional goal: clarity and momentum.** No gamification. No gentle nudging. Respect for the user's time and intelligence.

---

## Brand Personality

**Precise. Accountable. Uncluttered.**

The copy already signals it: "the app that helps you stay honest." This is not hustle culture. It's not aspirational startup pink-and-blue. It's a serious tool for serious people — warm through restraint, not through color or animation. Closer to a sharp editor's toolkit than a productivity coach's dashboard.

---

## Aesthetic Direction

**Dark monochromatic is a core commitment, not a fashion choice.** It signals to the user that this is a serious tool. Protect it.

**The one evolution: a single semantic accent color.** Committed color: `#E8A838` — a cool amber that reads as achievement warmth without crossing into celebration. This is a utility color, not a brand color. It appears in roughly 5% of the interface — streaks, completion events, AI-generated content surfaces. Enough to mean something when it does. Nowhere else.

**Anti-references — what TaskFlow must not look like:**

- Notion's soft minimalism (too passive, too beige)
- Todoist's chirpy productivity (bouncy, patronizing)
- Pastel gamification (streaks as confetti)
- Startup pink-and-blue gradients (gradient text, glowing cards)

**References — what to study:**

- **Arc browser** — focused, opinionated, premium without being cold
- **Linear** — velocity, respect for the user's time, surgical spacing
- **Early Superhuman** — takes the user seriously, every pixel justified

**Weakest area to fix:** the Journal page and any surface where AI output appears inline. Text-on-text in a monochromatic system has no visual weight — AI-sourced content blends into the interface and loses meaning. This is where the accent earns its place: a faint amber-tinted left rule or background shift, not a color block, signals that this content came from somewhere else.

---

## Motion Language

Motion is functional, not expressive. Transitions exist to orient, not to delight. Default to 80–120ms eases. Nothing bounces. Nothing lingers. Completion events can pulse once — they do not celebrate. If a transition could be cut without the user noticing, cut it.

---

## Design Principles

1. **Command center, not cheerleader.** Every surface should feel like it gives the user control and orientation — never encouragement theater.

2. **Restraint as signal.** The dark monochromatic palette communicates seriousness. The accent color is a statement — it appears only when something meaningful occurred.

3. **Hierarchy through precision, not decoration.** Weight, size, spacing, and opacity do the work. No cards-on-cards. No gradient text. No decorative borders.

4. **AI has a voice, not a costume.** AI-generated content gets visual distinction through weight or texture — a subtle amber rule, a tighter type treatment — not a different color scheme. The signal is "this came from somewhere else," not "this is a different product."

5. **One feature at a time feels complete.** Each page should feel resolved, not half-finished. No feature should look like an afterthought.
