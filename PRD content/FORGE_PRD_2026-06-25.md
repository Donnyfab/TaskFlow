# Forge PRD

Date: 2026-06-25  
Status: Draft for product, landing page, and MVP alignment  
Product: Forge, formerly TaskFlow / tflow.live

## 1. Product Summary

Forge is a personal execution coach for ambitious builders who have goals but struggle to convert intention into consistent action.

Forge is not a regular task app, habit tracker, or chatbot. It turns goals into commitments, asks for proof, follows up after deadlines, remembers behavior, and helps users build discipline through evidence-based progress.

Core promise:

> Forge helps users become the kind of person who follows through.

## 2. Current Product State

The current codebase already includes a significant Forge foundation:

- Flask backend in `app.py` with auth, sessions, tasks, habits, notes, calendar, focus, settings, Coach APIs, push notification APIs, and Forge-specific mission / commitment / output endpoints.
- Next.js frontend in `frontend/` with protected app routes for Coach, Mission, Commitments, Output Log, Patterns, Settings, Tasks, Habits, Calendar, Focus, and Journal.
- Public Flask landing page in `templates/landing_page.html` styled around Forge, plus supporting static assets in `static/`.
- Forge Coach flow in `frontend/src/app/(app)/ai/page.tsx`.
- Forge dashboard surfaces in `frontend/src/components/forge/ForgePage.tsx`.
- Onboarding persistence in `forge_onboarding.py`.
- Coach memory, commitment check-ins, pattern detection, roadmap summaries, weekly summaries, adaptive profile, and retention nudges in `forge_memory.py`.
- Commitment capture, due commitment check-ins, proof levels, and check-in outcome handling in `forge_coach.py`.
- Database migrations for missions, commitments, outputs, proof metadata, pattern events, memory, push subscriptions, onboarding fields, and accountability metadata.

Current gap:

The product has many of the right pieces, but the experience needs to become one coherent Forge loop:

Goal -> Coach intake -> Mission -> Commitment -> Deadline -> Follow-up -> Proof -> Progress -> Next commitment -> Memory.

The landing page also needs to communicate this loop clearly. It should not look like a generic SaaS or productivity template.

## 3. Product Positioning

### One-Liner

Forge is an AI execution coach that turns goals into commitments, proof, and measurable progress.

### Expanded Positioning

Most productivity apps store tasks. Forge holds users accountable to the work they said mattered.

Forge helps users:

- Define the goal that actually matters.
- Break it into specific commitments.
- Attach deadlines and proof requirements.
- Report completion, partial completion, or missed execution.
- Reflect on behavior patterns.
- Build discipline through repeated evidence.

### Not This

Forge must not feel like:

- A generic AI chatbot.
- A normal task list.
- A habit streak toy.
- A motivational quote app.
- A cluttered life OS trying to do everything at once.
- A generic startup landing page with vague claims.

### Should Feel Like

- A serious personal execution coach.
- A private operating room for goals.
- A system that respects the user's ambition and time.
- Direct, calm, and useful.
- Premium, restrained, and uncluttered.

## 4. Target Users

Primary early users:

- Founders building a side project or startup.
- Creators trying to publish consistently.
- Developers shipping a product.
- Students building a future outside class.
- Young entrepreneurs trying to become more disciplined.
- Self-directed builders who know what they want but keep avoiding the hard action.

Best first beta profile:

18-30-year-old founders, creators, developers, or students with one project they keep avoiding.

## 5. Core Problem

Users do not only need another place to write tasks.

They struggle because:

- They confuse planning with progress.
- They overthink instead of shipping.
- They make vague commitments.
- They miss deadlines without reviewing why.
- They avoid proof because proof makes the result real.
- They start goals without a system that follows up.
- They lose context across tools.
- They use AI for advice but not execution.

Forge exists to close the gap between what the user says they want and what their behavior proves.

## 6. Core MVP Loop

The MVP succeeds only if this accountability loop works end to end:

1. User states a goal.
2. Forge understands the user's current situation.
3. Forge creates a mission.
4. Forge creates one concrete commitment.
5. Commitment has a deadline with date and time.
6. Commitment is saved.
7. Forge follows up after the deadline.
8. User reports kept, missed, or partial.
9. Forge asks for proof when appropriate.
10. Forge logs proof as output.
11. Forge updates progress and memory.
12. Forge creates the next commitment.
13. The loop repeats.

Principle:

> Forge should not reward intentions. Forge should reward evidence.

## 7. Immediate Priorities

### P0 - Deployment Health

Fix the current Vercel deployment issue before adding major new product surfaces.

Acceptance criteria:

- Production build succeeds.
- Landing page and authenticated app routes work.
- Login/session flow works across Flask and Next.
- No broken root or `/landing` routing behavior.
- Environment variables required by the app are documented.

### P0 - Coach as Product Front Door

Move onboarding into the Coach page and make Coach the new user's first real product experience.

Current direction already exists in `frontend/src/app/(app)/ai/page.tsx` and `forge_onboarding.py`; the goal is to finish and polish it.

Requirements:

- First-time authenticated users start onboarding inside Coach.
- Separate disconnected onboarding screens should redirect into Coach.
- Onboarding should end after roughly 6-8 meaningful user replies when enough data is captured.
- Forge must not ask endless intake questions.
- The completion point must be explicit and saved.
- Completion creates mission, first commitment, proof requirement, and memory.

Acceptance criteria:

- A new user can register, enter Coach, complete onboarding, and land in Forge with a mission and first commitment.
- Onboarding cannot complete without a concrete commitment, deadline, and proof requirement.
- If saving fails, the user sees a useful retry state and does not lose the conversation.

### P0 - Accountability Spine

Finish the commitment/proof/check-in loop.

Requirements:

- Active commitment is visible on Coach and Mission.
- Due commitments trigger a clear check-in:
  - "You said you would [commitment] by [deadline]. Did you do it?"
- User can answer:
  - Yes: log win, request proof if required, create next commitment.
  - No: ask what happened, detect avoidance, shrink the next action.
  - Partially: clarify what got done, log partial proof, close the gap.
- Kept and partial commitments require proof before resolution when the commitment represents real output.
- Missed commitments update memory and pattern detection.

Acceptance criteria:

- A saved commitment appears in the Commitments view.
- Resolving kept/partial creates an output when proof is submitted.
- Missed commitments increase accountability history and update Coach context.
- Coach references the latest commitment state accurately.

## 9. Product Requirements

### 9.1 Coach

Coach is the product's front door and daily accountability surface.

Requirements:

- Start onboarding for new users.
- Ask one question at a time.
- Keep replies short and direct.
- Detect avoidance patterns without overdiagnosing.
- Confirm the pattern before treating it as final.
- Create commitments only after the user accepts a specific action.
- Refuse vague commitments like "soon", "maybe", or "this week" without exact date/time.
- Ask for proof when the commitment produces a visible output.
- Remember previous commitments, misses, outputs, and patterns.

Key principle:

Coach should help, challenge, summarize, plan, commit, and finish. It should not run an endless interview.

### 9.2 Onboarding

Onboarding should collect:

- Name.
- Desired identity.
- Current struggle.
- Avoided task.
- What matters now.
- Fall-off trigger.
- Mission.
- Outcome.
- Obstacle.
- Pattern label.
- Identity gap.
- Mission deadline.
- First commitment.
- Commitment deadline.
- Why the commitment matters.
- Proof required.
- Proof level.

Output of onboarding:

- Active mission.
- First commitment.
- Coach memory summary.
- Initial pattern label.
- Dashboard ready state.

### 9.3 Commitments

Commitments are the atomic unit of accountability.

Each commitment must include:

- Commitment text.
- Deadline with date and time.
- Status: pending, kept, missed, partial.
- Related mission.
- Why it matters.
- Proof requirement.
- Proof level: low, medium, high.
- Progress: not started, in progress, blocked, done.
- Follow-up time / last follow-up time.

Rules:

- Commitments must be concrete enough that another person could verify them.
- High-proof commitments should request file, link, image, video, document, or public evidence.
- Low-proof commitments can allow written confirmation when the work is minor.
- Missed commitments should produce a smaller next action, not shame.

### 9.4 Proof-Based Execution

Proof types:

- Image.
- Video.
- URL.
- GitHub link.
- Figma link.
- Website.
- Uploaded file.
- Document / PDF.
- Written reflection.

Proof levels:

- Low: simple confirmation.
- Medium: short reflection or description.
- High: file, image, URL, document, or visible shipped artifact.

Rules:

- Do not require proof for every small habit.
- Require proof when the task produces an output.
- Proof should create an output log entry.

### 9.5 Output Log / Evidence Vault

The output log is permanent evidence of progress.

Possible user-facing names:

- Evidence Vault.
- Mission Archive.
- Progress Story.
- Journey.

Each entry should include:

- Date.
- Mission.
- Related commitment.
- Proof.
- Proof level.
- Coach feedback.
- User reflection.
- What changed next.

Requirements:

- Outputs should feed back into Coach memory.
- Coach should reference prior proof when useful.
- The archive should show user transformation over time.

Example Coach references:

- "You solved this same blocker three months ago."
- "You have submitted 15 outreach messages before."
- "This looks like the challenge you overcame in April."

### 9.6 Mission / Roadmap

Forge should plan with the user, not only chat.

MVP requirements:

- Mission exists after onboarding.
- Mission has outcome, obstacle, deadline, and active status.
- Roadmap summary can be generated from mission, commitments, outputs, and current behavior.
- Roadmap should show current position, next milestone, stages, risks, and output progress.

Later requirements:

- Saved roadmaps.
- Editable milestones.
- Checkable steps.
- Persistent roadmap changes.
- Automatic updates based on behavior.
- Research-informed roadmap creation for major goals.

### 9.7 Research-Informed Roadmaps

This is not P0.

Use research only for major roadmap creation or major roadmap updates.

Flow:

1. User confirms major goal.
2. Coach asks motive and current situation.
3. Research agent studies the domain.
4. Research agent briefs Coach.
5. Planner agent creates roadmap.
6. Coach explains it naturally.
7. User confirms or adjusts.
8. Roadmap saves to database.

Do not dump research on the user. Convert research into a clear plan.

### 9.8 Personalized Dashboard

After onboarding, the dashboard should not be blank.

It should show:

- Mission.
- Today's task / active commitment.
- Deadline.
- Roadmap.
- Coach summary.
- Behavior patterns.
- Commitment history.
- Output log.
- Weekly progress.
- Recent wins.
- Current blocker.
- Next check-in.

Flow:

Conversation -> extracted knowledge -> saved memory -> mission -> roadmap -> dashboard -> daily coaching.

### 9.9 Settings

Settings should let users calibrate Forge without making it fake.

Settings to support:

- Coach tone: direct, balanced, supportive / firm support.
- Message length: short, normal, detailed.
- Accountability strictness: light, standard, strict.
- Proof requirement preference.
- Check-in frequency.
- Reminder channels.
- Goal focus.
- Adaptive mode on/off.
- Notification style.
- Communication style.

Existing settings and tone calibration should be expanded carefully.

### 9.10 Phone / iMessage Coach

Future feature.

Principle:

Forge app is headquarters. Phone/iMessage is the field agent.

App:

- Think.
- Reflect.
- Plan.
- Review.
- Work.

Phone/iMessage:

- Check in.
- Follow up.
- Remind.
- Hold accountable.
- Send reports.
- Handle quick replies.

Do not prioritize this before the web accountability loop is reliable.

## 10. Testing Requirements

### Persistent User Simulation Agents

Use persistent simulation agents to test whether Forge handles real behavior over time.

Core personas:

- Drifter: vague, changes direction, needs clarity.
- Dreamer: ambitious, over-plans, avoids execution.
- Builder: consistent but needs better strategy.
- Burned-Out Operator: ambitious but overwhelmed.

Jordan simulation:

- Young teenager.
- Wants to build a business.
- Unclear goals.
- Inconsistent.
- Defensive.
- Procrastinates.
- Slowly improves if Forge works.

Run a 14-day simulation and report:

- Where Forge helped.
- Where Forge failed.
- Repeated avoidance patterns.
- Coach weaknesses.
- Product improvements needed.

Daily scoring:

- Did Forge understand the goal? 1-10.
- Was the roadmap useful? 1-10.
- Did Forge get the user to take action?
- Did Forge remember previous conversations?
- Did Forge call out excuses accurately?
- Would the user come back tomorrow?
- Biggest frustration.
- Biggest improvement.
- Did the user complete the commitment?
- Did Forge adapt?
- At what point would the user have quit?

### Human Beta Testing

Start with 10 real users, then 25, 50, 100, and 500 paying users.

Measure:

- Day 2 return rate.
- Day 7 return rate.
- Commitments created.
- Commitments resolved.
- Proof submitted.
- Outputs logged.
- Goal completion.
- Drop-off point.
- Why users did not return.
- What made users come back.

Do not ask "Do you like Forge?"

Ask:

- Why did you not open it yesterday?
- What felt annoying?
- What made you come back?
- What stopped you from completing the task?

## 16. Definition of Done for MVP

Forge MVP is done when:

- A new user signs up and starts inside Coach.
- Coach completes onboarding without endless questions.
- The user leaves onboarding with a mission, first commitment, deadline, and proof requirement.
- The dashboard is populated from the onboarding conversation.
- Forge follows up after the commitment deadline.
- The user can resolve the commitment as kept, missed, or partial.
- Kept/partial output commitments require proof.
- Proof creates an output log entry.
- Coach remembers the result and creates the next commitment.
- The landing page clearly explains why Forge is not a normal task app.
